Kazakh Delicacies

Discover the unique and flavorful traditional foods of Kazakhstan

Traditional Kazakh Cuisine

Kazakh cuisine reflects the country's nomadic heritage and harsh climate. Traditionally, Kazakh food centered around meat and dairy products, with horse meat and mutton being particularly important. The nomadic lifestyle required food that was easy to prepare and provided substantial energy for life on the steppes.

Today, while maintaining its traditional roots, Kazakh cuisine has also incorporated influences from Russian, Uzbek, Uyghur, and other Central Asian culinary traditions. Here we present some of the most beloved and distinctive Kazakh delicacies that every visitor should try.

Main Dishes

Beshbarmak

The national dish of Kazakhstan, Beshbarmak (meaning "five fingers" as it's traditionally eaten with the hands) consists of boiled horse meat or mutton served over large, flat noodles and topped with a flavorful onion sauce. The meat is typically boiled for several hours to create a rich broth, which is served alongside the dish in small bowls called "sorpa."

Where to try: Traditional Kazakh restaurants in Nur-Sultan and Almaty, or for an authentic experience, in a yurt restaurant outside the cities.

Kazy

Kazy is a traditional Kazakh sausage made from horse meat, specifically from the rib area. The meat is seasoned with garlic, pepper, and other spices, then stuffed into the horse's intestine and either boiled or smoked. Kazy is often served sliced as an appetizer or as part of the main Beshbarmak dish.

Where to try: Local markets, traditional restaurants, or during special celebrations and festivals.

Plov

While originally from Uzbekistan, Plov has become a staple in Kazakh cuisine. This hearty rice dish is made with meat (usually lamb), carrots, onions, and various spices. The Kazakh version often includes more meat and fewer vegetables than its Uzbek counterpart. Plov is typically prepared in a large cast-iron pot called a "kazan."

Where to try: Any Kazakh restaurant, particularly those specializing in Central Asian cuisine.

Kuurdak

Kuurdak is a traditional dish made from fried meat (usually lamb or beef) with potatoes and onions. The meat is cut into small pieces and fried until crispy, then combined with the vegetables and spices. This hearty dish was popular among nomads due to its simplicity and the ability to prepare it quickly over an open fire.

Where to try: Local eateries and family-run restaurants throughout Kazakhstan.

Breads & Snacks

Baursak

Baursak are small pieces of fried dough, similar to donuts but usually unsweetened. These puffy, golden bread pieces are a staple at Kazakh celebrations and are typically served with tea. They can be eaten with honey or jam for a sweet treat, or alongside savory dishes.

Shelpek

Shelpek is a traditional flatbread that holds cultural significance in Kazakh cuisine. It's often prepared for special occasions and religious ceremonies. The dough is rolled thin and fried in oil, resulting in a crispy exterior and soft interior. Shelpek is typically round and can be either sweet or savory.

Samsa

Samsa are baked pastries filled with meat (usually lamb), onions, and spices. Similar to samosas found in other cultures, Kazakh samsa are typically triangular or square in shape and baked in a tandoor oven, giving them a distinctive flavor and crispy texture. They make for a perfect portable snack or appetizer.

Kurt

Kurt is a traditional dried cheese made from fermented milk. These small, hard balls or chunks have a salty, sour taste and were historically important for nomadic Kazakhs as they could be preserved for long periods. Kurt is still popular today as a snack and is often enjoyed with beer or added to soups for flavor.

Traditional Beverages

Kumys

Kumys is fermented mare's milk and has been a staple of nomadic Kazakh culture for centuries. Slightly alcoholic (about 2%) with a tangy, sour taste, it's considered to have health benefits and is especially popular during summer months. Traditionally, it was prepared in leather bags that were periodically beaten to ensure proper fermentation.

Shubat

Similar to kumys but made from camel's milk, shubat is a fermented dairy drink with a distinctive taste. It's thicker than kumys and has a slightly higher fat content. Shubat is particularly common in the western and southern regions of Kazakhstan where camels are more prevalent.

Ayran

Ayran is a refreshing yogurt-based drink made by mixing yogurt with water and salt. This cooling beverage is especially popular during hot summer months. Unlike kumys and shubat, ayran is non-alcoholic and has a milder taste, making it more accessible to those unaccustomed to fermented dairy products.

Kazakh Food Etiquette

Food plays a central role in Kazakh hospitality, and there are several customs and traditions surrounding meals that visitors should be aware of:

Hospitality Customs

  • Refusing food or drink offered by a Kazakh host is considered impolite. At least taste a little of what is offered.
  • The most honored guest is usually seated furthest from the door.
  • The host will often serve the best pieces of meat to guests as a sign of respect.
  • Traditional meals often begin with tea and end with more tea.
  • It's customary to offer a blessing (bata) before or after the meal.

Dining Etiquette

  • Wash your hands before eating, as many traditional dishes are eaten with the hands.
  • When eating Beshbarmak, use your right hand or both hands, never just your left hand.
  • The eldest or most respected person at the table is usually served first.
  • It's polite to try everything that is served.
  • Leave a small amount of food on your plate to show that you've had enough and were well-fed.

Where to Experience Kazakh Cuisine

Top Restaurants

  • Gakku Restaurant (Nur-Sultan & Almaty)

    Upscale dining featuring traditional Kazakh dishes with a modern twist.

  • Navat (Multiple Locations)

    Chain restaurant offering authentic Central Asian cuisine in a traditional setting.

  • Alasha (Almaty)

    Known for its theatrical atmosphere and traditional Kazakh and Uzbek dishes.

  • Kishlak (Nur-Sultan)

    Rustic restaurant specializing in authentic Kazakh cuisine and shashlik.

Unique Dining Experiences

  • Yurt Dining

    Several tour operators offer traditional meals in authentic yurts outside major cities.

  • Green Bazaar (Almaty)

    Sample local delicacies and purchase traditional foods at this bustling market.

  • Home-Stay Meals

    Experience authentic home-cooked Kazakh meals by arranging a dinner with a local family through tour agencies.

  • Seasonal Festivals

    Visit during Nauryz (spring equinox) to experience special holiday foods and celebrations.
